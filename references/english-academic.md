# English academic rewriting guide

Read this file when the text is mostly English and the request is about humanizing, de-templating, or lowering detector-prone academic signals. For ordinary English academic polishing, copyediting, proofreading, grammar correction, or clarity improvement, read `polishing.md` first and use this file only for detector-prone patterns.

## Keep these constraints

- Preserve terminology, citations, equations, and claim strength.
- Keep the register academic. Do not turn a paper into a blog post.
- Use simple verbs such as `is`, `has`, and `shows` when they are the clearest choice.
- Do not inject personality or first person unless the source text or the user's sample clearly supports it.

## Prioritize these fixes

### 1. Aim boilerplate and ceremonial framing

Risk patterns:

- `This study aims to`
- `This paper seeks to`
- `The purpose of this study is to`

Prefer:

- State the action directly.
- Keep one aim statement if the genre truly needs it, but avoid repeating it.

### 2. Significance inflation

Risk patterns:

- `critical`
- `vital`
- `pivotal`
- `profound implications`
- `broader landscape`

Prefer:

- Replace inflated significance with scope, mechanism, or observed effect.
- If the adjective adds no information, cut it.

### 3. Vague attributions

Risk patterns:

- `studies have shown`
- `experts believe`
- `it is widely accepted`
- `research indicates`

Prefer:

- Cite specifically if a source is already present.
- Otherwise own the sentence or remove it.

### 4. Present-participle padding

Risk patterns:

- `..., highlighting ...`
- `..., underscoring ...`
- `..., reflecting ...`
- `..., contributing to ...`

Prefer:

- Split the clause into a real sentence.
- Keep only the part that adds actual content.

### 5. Rule-of-three symmetry

Risk patterns:

- repeated triads in sentences or paragraphs
- `first, second, third` structure everywhere
- balanced three-item conclusions

Prefer:

- Use two points when two are enough.
- Give uneven emphasis so the prose does not sound machine-balanced.

### 6. Elegant variation

Risk patterns:

- rotating among `participants`, `subjects`, `respondents`, and `individuals` without a change in meaning
- renaming the same concept every paragraph

Prefer:

- Keep one stable term when precision matters.

### 7. Copula avoidance

Risk patterns:

- `serves as`
- `functions as`
- `acts as`
- `plays a role in`

Prefer:

- Use `is`, `has`, or `does` when that is all the sentence means.

### 8. Contrast theatrics

Risk patterns:

- `not only ... but also`
- `it is not X but Y`
- heavy em-dash staging

Prefer:

- Say the point directly.
- Reduce staging devices unless the sentence genuinely needs contrast.

### 9. Generic conclusions

Risk patterns:

- `future research should explore`
- `important implications`
- `opens new avenues`
- `highlights the importance of`

Prefer:

- Name the actual limit, next test, or practical implication already supported by the text.

### 10. Flat detector-friendly rhythm

Risk patterns:

- uniform mid-length sentences
- repetitive transitions at paragraph openings
- every paragraph ending with the same type of takeaway sentence

Prefer:

- Vary sentence length modestly.
- Remove transition scaffolding where adjacency already carries the logic.

## Quick replacements

| Risky phrase | Prefer |
| --- | --- |
| `It is important to note that` | delete |
| `In the context of` | `In` / direct statement |
| `The findings underscore` | `The findings show` / `The findings suggest` |
| `plays a pivotal role` | state the actual function |
| `future research should explore` | name the unresolved test or limit |
| `not only ... but also` | direct statement with one clear emphasis |

## Section tips

- Abstract: keep aim, method, result, and takeaway; cut rhetorical framing.
- Literature review: synthesize patterns or disagreements instead of listing authors in sequence.
- Methods: prefer operational precision over polished abstraction.
- Results: say what changed and by how much.
- Discussion: admit uncertainty where the evidence is limited.
- Conclusion: stop before the prose turns into mission language.
